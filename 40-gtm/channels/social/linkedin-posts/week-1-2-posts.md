## POST 1 — Blog Launch (Week 1)

**We wrote the guide we wish existed when we started.**

Customer discovery gets talked about a lot. But most guides either give you a 2-sentence definition or dump you into a 50-page academic framework.

Neither helps when you're a PM with 3 weeks to validate a bet.

So we wrote "What is Customer Discovery?" — the practical version.

The key insight we keep coming back to: **customer discovery isn't about collecting opinions. It's about collecting evidence.**

Opinions are "customers want better onboarding."
Evidence is a timestamped quote from your VP of Ops prospect saying "I lost two enterprise deals last quarter because setup took 6 weeks."

One gives you a direction. The other gives you proof you can take to your board, your investors, your eng team.

The full post covers what discovery actually is, why most teams do it wrong, and how to build a practice that produces insights you can act on — not just notes you forget.

## POST 2 — Pain Point Thread (Week 1)

**You talked to 20 customers last quarter.**

Your head of product asks: "What did we learn?"

You open your notes. Scroll through Google Docs. Skim some Notion pages. Maybe dig up a Miro board from that synthesis session in October.

You know the patterns are in there. You lived them. You heard the frustration in people's voices.

But you can't prove it.

You can't point to the 7 specific people who said onboarding was broken. You can't pull the exact quotes. You can't show which segment cares most.

So you paraphrase. You summarize from memory. You say "we heard a lot about..." and hope that carries weight.

It doesn't.

This is the evidence gap.

Teams are doing more customer research than ever. And retaining almost none of it in a form that's actually usable.

The conversations happen. The insights exist. But the proof evaporates.

Not because people are lazy. Because the tools weren't built for this.

Your CRM tracks deals. Your note app tracks text. Nothing tracks what your customers actually said and ties it to what you should do about it.

That's the gap we're closing.

## POST 3 — Behind the Scenes (Week 1)

**I have a weird obsession.**

I want to know where an insight came from.

Not "the team feels like..." Not "customers are saying..." I want the receipt.

Who said it. When they said it. What they actually said — word for word.

I've spent years watching good customer research disappear. A PM runs 12 interviews, writes a summary, and 3 months later nobody can trace a single product decision back to a real conversation.

That drives me crazy.

So we're building UpSight.

It's an intelligence layer for customer conversations. You talk to people — interviews, sales calls, stakeholder sessions — and UpSight pulls out the evidence automatically. Timestamped. Attributed. Searchable. Connected to themes and patterns across every conversation.

Not summaries. Not vibes. Receipts.

We're still early. Building in public when we can. But the core belief hasn't changed since day one:

**If you can't trace your insight back to a human being who said it, it's not an insight. It's a guess.**

## POST 4 — Consultant Pain Point (Week 2)

**You ran 15 stakeholder interviews for a client.**

Took meticulous notes. Spotted patterns. Built a mental model of what's really going on inside their organization.

Now the client asks: "What did you find?"

You open a Google Doc. 40 pages of notes. Color-coded if you were feeling ambitious. Maybe some bold text where something felt important.

You start scrolling. Scanning. Trying to reconstruct the narrative you had so clearly in your head two weeks ago.

The client watches you scroll.

This is not the moment you want to look uncertain.

But here's the thing — you're not uncertain. You know what you found. You just can't surface it fast enough, with enough specificity, to command the room.

Consultants live and die by credibility. And credibility comes from precision.

"7 of your 15 stakeholders independently described the handoff between sales and implementation as broken. Here are their exact words."

That lands differently than "we heard a lot about handoff issues."

The difference isn't the quality of your work. It's whether your evidence is accessible when you need it.

Your research deserves better than a Google Doc.

## POST 6 — Blog Link (Week 2)

**Hot take: most consulting deliverables are opinion laundering.**

Not intentionally. But when you can't trace a recommendation back to specific evidence from specific stakeholders, that's effectively what's happening.

We wrote about this — how the best consultants are shifting from narrative-driven to evidence-driven delivery.

The practical insight that stuck with me:

When you can show a client that 9 of 12 stakeholders independently raised the same issue — with their actual words — you stop having to convince them something is true. The evidence does it for you.

That changes the whole dynamic. You go from "here's what I think based on my experience" to "here's what your own people are telling us, organized so you can see it."

One positions you as an expert. The other positions you as indispensable.

The full post breaks down how evidence-based consulting works in practice — how to capture it, organize it, and present it in a way that makes clients trust the findings immediately.
